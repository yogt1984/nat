1. THESIS
================================================================================

Markets alternate between two regimes:

  Regime 0 — FAST mean-reversion (range-bound):
    Order book imbalance is quickly corrected by market makers refilling
    the depleted side. Prices oscillate around a stable level. Fade
    extremes, sell rallies, buy dips.

  Regime 1 — SLOW mean-reversion (trending):
    Directional flow overwhelms market maker replenishment. Imbalance
    persists and prices trend. Follow momentum, don't fight the flow.

The problem: you don't know which regime you're in. The switching OU
algorithm solves this by running TWO parallel Kalman filters (one tuned for
fast, one for slow mean-reversion) and using Bayesian inference (Hamilton
filter) to estimate the probability of being in each regime at every tick.

The trading signal is the regime-weighted state estimate:

  state = P(fast) * x_fast + P(slow) * x_slow

When the slow regime dominates (P(slow) -> 1), the state tracks a trending
imbalance — positive state means follow the trend upward. When the fast
regime dominates, the state reflects a transient imbalance that will revert.

The signal is traded as high_long: positive state = go long.

================================================================================
2. THE HIDDEN MARKOV MODEL
================================================================================

2.1  Two parallel OU processes
---------------------------------------------------------------------

  Regime 0 (fast):  theta_0 = 0.5   half-life ~ 1.4s
  Regime 1 (slow):  theta_1 = 0.05  half-life ~ 14s

  Both observe the same imbalance_qty_l1, but each Kalman filter
  interprets it through its own OU dynamics:

  Fast filter: "this imbalance is a temporary deviation, it will
               mean-revert quickly"
  Slow filter: "this imbalance is a persistent shift, it will
               take a long time to revert"

  The two filters produce different state estimates (x_fast, x_slow)
  and different innovations (innov_fast, innov_slow).

2.2  Transition matrix
---------------------------------------------------------------------

  The regime follows a Markov chain with per-tick transition probability:

    P = [[1 - rho,  rho  ],
         [rho,    1 - rho]]

  rho = 0.01 (default)

  Meaning: 1% chance of switching regime at each tick (100ms).
  Expected duration in each regime: 1/rho = 100 ticks = 10 seconds.

  The symmetric matrix means both regimes have equal persistence. This
  is a simplifying assumption — in practice, trending regimes may be
  shorter-lived than range-bound regimes.

2.3  Hamilton filter (Bayesian update)
---------------------------------------------------------------------

  At each tick, 4 steps:

  Step 1 — PRIOR (predict regime probabilities via transition matrix):
    pi_fast_prior = P[0,0] * pi_fast(t-1) + P[1,0] * pi_slow(t-1)
    pi_slow_prior = 1 - pi_fast_prior

  Step 2 — KALMAN UPDATE (run both filters on the new observation):
    (x_fast, P_fast, innov_fast) = KF_fast.step(z)
    (x_slow, P_slow, innov_slow) = KF_slow.step(z)

  Step 3 — LIKELIHOOD (how well does each regime explain the observation):
    L_fast = N(innov_fast; 0, P_fast)
    L_slow = N(innov_slow; 0, P_slow)

    The regime whose filter is less surprised by the observation gets a
    higher likelihood. If imbalance is reverting quickly, L_fast > L_slow.
    If imbalance persists, L_slow > L_fast.

  Step 4 — POSTERIOR (Bayes rule):
    pi_fast(t) = L_fast * pi_fast_prior / (L_fast * pi_fast_prior + L_slow * pi_slow_prior)

  This is the Hamilton (1989) filter — the standard method for online
  regime detection in hidden Markov models.

================================================================================
3. SIGNAL CONSTRUCTION
================================================================================

3.1  Regime-weighted state (alg_switching_ou_state) — PRIMARY
---------------------------------------------------------------------

  state(t) = pi_fast(t) * x_fast(t) + (1 - pi_fast(t)) * x_slow(t)

  This blends the two Kalman estimates by their regime probability.
  When the market is trending (pi_slow high), the slow filter's estimate
  dominates — it tracks the persistent imbalance direction.

3.2  Regime probability (alg_switching_ou_regime)
---------------------------------------------------------------------

  regime(t) = pi_fast(t)    in [0, 1]

  Close to 1.0: fast regime (mean-reverting, range-bound)
  Close to 0.0: slow regime (trending, directional)

  This is useful as a standalone regime indicator for gating other
  algorithms: only trade momentum signals when regime ~ 0, only trade
  mean-reversion signals when regime ~ 1.

3.3  Effective speed (alg_switching_ou_speed)
---------------------------------------------------------------------

  speed(t) = pi_fast(t) * theta_fast + (1 - pi_fast(t)) * theta_slow

  Blended mean-reversion speed. High speed (near 0.5) = fast regime.
  Low speed (near 0.05) = slow regime. Continuous interpolation.

3.4  Transition rate (alg_regime_transition_rate)
---------------------------------------------------------------------

  trate(t) = |pi_fast(t) - pi_fast(t-1)| / dt

  How fast the regime belief is changing. Spikes when the market flips
  between regimes. High transition rate = regime uncertainty = danger zone
  for any directional signal.

================================================================================
6. HOW IT DIFFERS FROM OTHER ALGORITHMS
================================================================================

  Algorithm           What it asks                         Method
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  kalman_imbalance    "Is imbalance persistent?"           Single Kalman
  optimal_entry       "Is imbalance STARTING to shift?"    SPRT on Kalman
  switching_ou        "WHICH REGIME is the market in?"     Two Kalman + HMM

  kalman_imbalance assumes one regime with fixed parameters.
  switching_ou admits the market has two modes and infers which one is
  active. This is a strictly richer model — it nests kalman_imbalance as
  a special case when pi_fast = 1 or pi_fast = 0 permanently.

  The cost of this richness: more parameters (theta_fast, theta_slow,
  transition_rate) and slower convergence (the Hamilton filter needs
  ~50 ticks to form a confident regime estimate after a switch).

================================================================================
8. RESEARCH DIRECTIONS
================================================================================

8.1  Use as a regime gate, not a standalone signal
  The most promising application may be as a filter for other algorithms:
    - Trade optimal_entry only when P(slow regime) > 0.7
      (imbalance is trending, entry timing matters)
    - Trade 3f_liquidity only when P(fast regime) > 0.7
      (mean-reverting regime, liquidity signals work)

  This is analogous to how spread_decomp gates hawkes_intensity —
  switching_ou provides regime context for timing algorithms.

8.2  Asymmetric transition rates
  Replace the symmetric transition matrix with:
    P[fast->slow] = rho_1
    P[slow->fast] = rho_2

  In practice, trending regimes (slow) tend to be shorter than
  range-bound regimes (fast). Learning rho_1 != rho_2 from data
  (via the adaptive_calibrator) could improve regime detection.

8.3  Three-regime model
  Add a third regime: VOLATILE (high theta, high sigma_process).
  This captures market microstructure around news events or liquidation
  cascades where imbalance is both fast-moving AND highly volatile.
  The current two-regime model conflates calm-fast with chaotic-fast.

8.4  Volume-dependent regime transitions
  Condition the transition matrix on volume: P(switch to trending) should
  be higher when volume spikes. Currently, rho is constant regardless of
  market conditions.

8.5  Cross-algorithm regime consensus
  Combine switching_ou regime with spread_decomp regime and hawkes
  excitement to build a composite regime indicator:
    - switching_ou says "trending"
    - spread_decomp says "low adverse selection"
    - hawkes says "high excitement"
    -> All three agree: high-conviction directional environment

8.6  Learn theta_fast and theta_slow from data
  The current defaults (0.5 and 0.05) are fixed. Use the adaptive
  calibrator to estimate per-symbol:
    - theta_fast from the autocorrelation of imbalance during
      low-volatility periods
    - theta_slow from the autocorrelation during high-volatility periods
  This adapts the regime definitions to each market's characteristics.
